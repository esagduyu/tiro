# Cicero to Tiro — A Letter (53 BC)

*From the letters of Marcus Tullius Cicero to his freedman and secretary,
Marcus Tullius Tiro. Public domain translation, adapted from the Loeb-era
English renderings of the Epistulae ad Familiares, Book XVI.*

---

I have received your letter, my dear Tiro, and I read in it all the affection
you bear me — the same affection I have known in you these many years. Your
health is the one thing I ask of the gods; recover it, and you will have given
me everything.

You tell me you are ashamed to have been so often ill, and to have been a
burden. Put the thought away. It is no burden to care for one who has cared for
so much of mine. How many things would have gone unremembered, how many words
unwritten, without your hand and your patience? You have preserved what I could
only speak. The oracle spoke, but without you the oracle was dumb.

Attend to yourself, then, and to nothing else. Do not hurry back on my account.
I would rather wait for you in good health than have you near me in poor. Read
when it rests you, and set the work aside when it does not. There will be time
enough, and books enough, for both of us.

Take care of yourself. That is the whole of my letter, and the whole of my
wish.

---

Tiro outlived Cicero by many years, edited and published his master's letters
and speeches, and — by tradition — gave his name to the shorthand later scribes
called *notae Tironianae*. The little mark **⁊**, still used for *and* in some
scripts, is his. This application carries his name for the same reason: to keep
what matters, and to make it easy to find again.
